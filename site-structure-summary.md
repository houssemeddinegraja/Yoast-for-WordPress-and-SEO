# Site Structure — Simple Summary

## Why Site Structure Matters

Without a clear structure, your site is just a random pile of pages and posts. Structure matters for two reasons: **usability** (can people actually find what they want?) and **SEO** (can Google understand and rank your site?).

### Importance for Usability
A good structure helps visitors navigate easily and quickly grasp what your site is about or sells. Categorizing your content properly increases the chance visitors become customers.

### Importance for SEO
A solid structure boosts your ranking chances for three key reasons:
1. **Helps Google understand your site** — structure gives Google clues about your most valuable content, and how quickly it gets found and indexed.
2. **Prevents you from competing with yourself** — if you have several similar articles, Google won't know which one matters most. Good internal linking + taxonomy tells Google which page to prioritize, instead of the pages competing against each other.
3. **Handles change over time** — as your content/products evolve, a good structure helps you manage what to keep, update, or remove, so Google doesn't keep showing outdated stuff.

## How to Build Your Site Structure

**Categories**
Divide your posts/pages into categories (e.g., "Books," "Food," "Music," "Travel"). Every post needs at least one category. Categories can have subcategories (e.g., "Books" → "Fiction," "Nonfiction"), and each category should be roughly the same size.

**Tags**
Tags describe a post in more detail than categories, but — unlike categories — they have no hierarchy and are entirely optional (e.g., under "Food," tags could be "desserts," "pasta," "pizza," "salads"). Using both categories and tags together helps people find content more easily.

**Internal Linking**
Identify your most critical content (your "cornerstone" articles — see below) and link to them from your other, related blog posts. Linking between closely related pages helps search engines understand what's important on your site.

## Cornerstone Content

**What is it?**
Cornerstone articles are the most important pages on your site — the ones that reflect your company's core mission, and that you most want to rank for.

**Which articles should be cornerstones?**
Pick 4–5 pages you'd want a first-time visitor to read. These should form the foundation of your site's main topics. They should be:
- Lengthy and comprehensive
- Timeless (evergreen) and regularly updated
- Genuinely informative — even for a webshop, cornerstone content should educate (e.g., explain product uniqueness or use), not just sell.

**Why cornerstone content matters**
Popular/competitive search terms are hard to rank for. A cornerstone approach helps you signal to Google which article is your authority on a topic, especially when you have several posts about similar things.

**Where should it live?**
Ideally, a visitor should be able to click from your homepage straight to a cornerstone article. All your other related posts should link to it — this internal linking structure boosts its ranking chances.

## Cornerstone Content Tools in Yoast SEO

Yoast SEO offers tools to support both requirements of a good cornerstone strategy (being well-optimized *and* well-linked):

- **Text link counter** — filter cornerstone articles in your Posts overview and see how many internal links each one receives/sends, so you can spot if a cornerstone article isn't getting enough internal links.
- **Cornerstone analysis** — mark any post as "cornerstone content" in the Yoast meta box/sidebar. This makes both the SEO and readability checks *stricter*, since cornerstone content is held to a higher standard (longer, better-written).
- **Internal linking suggestions (Premium)** — suggests 5 relevant internal links (posts, pages, tags, categories) while you write, with cornerstone articles shown first so you don't forget to link to them.
- **Internal linking blocks (Premium)** — four block types to automate internal linking:
  - *Related links* — pre-filled with suggested related links.
  - *Table of contents* — auto-generated from your headings, updates automatically.
  - *Siblings* — lists other pages sharing the same parent page.
  - *Subpages* — lists all pages underneath the current parent page.

## Key Takeaways

- Structuring your site is essential for both usability and findability.
- Use categories, subcategories, and tags to build a solid structure.
- Great cornerstone content is lengthy, well-written, and well-optimized.
- Cornerstone content needs a prominent place in your site's structure — link to it often.
- Yoast SEO provides built-in tools to help you execute a proper cornerstone content strategy.
